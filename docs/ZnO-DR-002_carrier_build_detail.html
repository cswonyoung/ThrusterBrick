1 · Build philosophy

Every carrier is a small stack of flat plates — a solid layer, a windowed layer, and where a face must seal, an O-ring dropped into a routed groove — bolted together with bought hardware. Nothing is a freeform 3-D shape, so the router makes every structural part and the rest is a purchase. Two of the three carriers share one architecture (a window/mask sandwich); the strip carrier is its inverse with a spring-loaded rim clamp.

2 · Material map — polymer & seal change by zone

This is the part most likely to bite: PVDF is excellent in your acids, silver, and oxidisers but is attacked by strong hot NaOH, and FKM/Viton O-rings fail in caustic. So the strip carrier drops to polypropylene with EPDM seals, while everything upstream stays PVDF/FKM.

PTFE is inert everywhere but soft and poor at holding threads — reserve it for standoffs, faces, and universal spacers. PEEK is the high-temperature, thread-holding upgrade. Standoffs are PTFE throughout since it is compatible with every bath.

3 · Immersion & seeding carriers — the window/mask sandwich

Exploded, face-down. Back/mask plate over the coupon, FKM O-ring in the window-plate groove, active-area window open downward, PTFE standoff legs (unequal for tilt).

The coupon is clamped between a solid back/mask plate and a window plate; the window frames the active area and the O-ring seals the border so only the working face (through the window) meets the bath. Reused for both carriers — orientation and material change, architecture does not.

4 · Strip edge-clamp carrier — the inverse, spring-clamped

Exploded, face-up. Solid PP backer protects the high-P face; coupon zinc-side up; EPDM O-ring at the 13 mm rim; windowed clamp frame with Ti Belleville springs holding even pressure.

Inverse of the sandwich: the solid backer is on the bottom (protecting the high-P face from caustic), the window is on top (zinc face exposed so H₂ escapes), and the clamp lands exactly on the 13 mm rim. Spring washers keep pressure even as things settle in hot NaOH.

5 · Wafer bonding chuck — not a carrier

A flat vacuum chuck for the Phase-1 die-attach: route a shallow ⌀150.5 wafer recess, two concentric vacuum rings + a cross groove, and a centre port into a rigid plate (½″ MIC-6 aluminium), line the recess with a clean silicone-free compliant pad, and pull vacuum from a bought source. The wafer sits polished-face-down on the pad while the foil is laid onto its rough (up) side. Router makes the recess, grooves, and port; buy a venturi vacuum generator or small pump, tubing/fittings, and the chuck pad. Flatness of this chuck protects the foil and limits CTE bow during cure, so surface it flat on the router. The old butyl-land film mask and PVDF clamp frame are retired — see ZnO-DR-006.

7 · Router & fabrication notes

OperationNote
Windows & profiles2.5-D profile from stock plate; the window inner dimension sets the active area — hold it tight.
O-ring groovesRoute with a small square/ball endmill; depth ≈ 0.7× cord dia, width ≈ 1.3× cord dia for ~25 % squeeze (standard face-seal gland).
Cord spliceThe groove is now a circle — splice the O-ring cord anywhere on the ring; there are no corners (the old "never on a corner" rule is retired).
ThreadsTap plastic directly with a hand tap, or through-bolt + nut; avoid metal threaded inserts in wetted/reactive zones.
Stock gaugesDesign each layer to a purchasable plate thickness — thickness is an order, not a machining step (same discipline as the cell plates).
PP machiningSharp single-flute tooling and dust/chip clearance; PP is gummy but cuts clean with the right feeds.
DeburrDeburr all routed plastic edges so O-rings seat and foils are not nicked.
